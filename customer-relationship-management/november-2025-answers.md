# Customer Relationship Management - Answers

## Q1

**a) Define CRM and explain the emergence of customer centric approach in changing face of CRM.**

**Answer:**

Customer Relationship Management (CRM) is a technology-driven strategy to manage a company's relationships and interactions with potential and existing customers. It involves using data analysis to study large amounts of information and then using it to improve business relationships, specifically focusing on customer retention and ultimately driving sales growth.

The emergence of the customer-centric approach has fundamentally changed the face of CRM. Initially, CRM was more of a transactional tool focused on sales automation. However, with rising competition and more informed customers, businesses realized the importance of building long-term relationships. This led to a shift from a product-centric to a customer-centric model. Instead of "what can we sell them?", the question became "what do our customers need?". This approach places the customer at the core of all business decisions, from product development to marketing and sales. CRM systems evolved to support this by providing a 360-degree view of the customer, enabling personalized communication, proactive service, and a consistent customer experience across all touchpoints.

**b) Mention and explain various phases of value chain model.**

**Answer:**

The value chain model, proposed by Michael Porter, describes the full range of activities required to bring a product or service from its conception to its end use and beyond. It is divided into primary and support activities. The phases (primary activities) are:

1.  **Inbound Logistics:** This involves the receiving, storing, and inventory control of raw materials. For a manufacturing company, this would be the process of acquiring raw materials for production.
2.  **Operations:** These are the activities that transform inputs into the final product. This includes machining, packaging, assembly, and equipment maintenance.
3.  **Outbound Logistics:** These activities are associated with the distribution of the final product to customers. It includes warehousing, order fulfillment, and transportation.
4.  **Marketing and Sales:** This phase involves the activities that persuade customers to purchase a product or service. This includes advertising, promotion, sales-force management, and pricing.
5.  **Service:** This includes all the activities required to keep the product working effectively for the buyer after it is sold and delivered. This includes installation, training, maintenance, and repair.

**c) Explain how customization helps in building relationships?**

**Answer:**

Customization is the process of tailoring products, services, and communications to meet the specific needs and preferences of individual customers. It is a powerful tool for building strong customer relationships in several ways:

**e) Write short note on Customer Life time Value.**

**Answer:**

Customer Lifetime Value (CLV or CLTV) is a metric that represents the total net profit a company can expect to generate from a single customer account throughout their entire relationship with the company. It's a prediction of the value your relationship with a customer can bring to your business.

Calculating CLV involves considering the customer's average purchase value, the frequency of their purchases, and their expected lifespan as a customer. The basic formula is:

*CLV = (Average Purchase Value) x (Number of Purchases Per Year) x (Average Customer Lifespan in Years)*

CLV is a crucial metric because it shifts the focus from short-term profits to long-term customer relationships. By understanding which customers are most valuable over time, a company can tailor its marketing and retention efforts to maximize profitability. For example, a company might invest more in retaining high-CLV customers, even if it means lower short-term profits.

**a) Define Customer Portfolio Management and enlist its Key Components.**

**Answer:**

Customer Portfolio Management (CPM) is the process of categorizing a company's customers into different groups based on their value and needs, and then managing each group with a tailored strategy to maximize overall profitability. It's about understanding that not all customers are equally profitable and that different customer segments require different levels of investment and attention.

**Key Components of Customer Portfolio Management:**

1.  **Customer Segmentation:** This is the process of dividing customers into distinct groups based on characteristics such as profitability, potential value, demographics, behavior, or needs. A common method is to use RFM analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary value).
2.  **Customer Valuation:** This involves assessing the current and potential value of each customer segment. This is often done using metrics like Customer Lifetime Value (CLV).
3.  **Strategy Development:** Based on the customer segmentation and valuation, tailored strategies are developed for each segment. For example, high-value customers might receive a dedicated account manager and exclusive offers, while low-value customers might be served through a self-service portal.
4.  **Performance Measurement:** This involves tracking the performance of the CPM strategy by monitoring key metrics such as customer retention, churn rate, and profitability for each segment.
5.  **Resource Allocation:** Based on the value of each customer segment, resources (time, money, and personnel) are allocated accordingly. The goal is to invest more in the most profitable segments.

**b) Write short note on Sales Forecasting.**

**Answer:**

Sales forecasting is the process of estimating future sales revenue over a specific period, such as a month, quarter, or year. It is a crucial activity for any business as it impacts budgeting, production planning, and resource allocation. Accurate sales forecasts enable companies to make informed business decisions and predict short-term and long-term performance.

There are several methods for sales forecasting, which can be broadly categorized as qualitative and quantitative.

*   **Qualitative Methods:** These are based on expert opinions and subjective judgments. Examples include the Delphi method, sales force composite, and customer surveys.
*   **Quantitative Methods:** These use historical sales data and statistical techniques to predict future sales. Examples include time series analysis (e.g., moving averages, exponential smoothing) and causal modeling (e.g., regression analysis).

Modern CRM systems often have built-in sales forecasting tools that automate much of this process by analyzing the sales pipeline, historical data, and other relevant factors.

**c) Explain how internal and external environment are examined by SWOT Analysis.**

**Answer:**

SWOT analysis is a strategic planning framework used to identify and analyze an organization's Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. It provides a comprehensive overview of the internal and external factors that can impact a business.

*   **Internal Environment (Strengths and Weaknesses):** The internal environment consists of factors within the organization that can be controlled.
    *   **Strengths:** These are the internal attributes and resources that give the organization a competitive advantage. Examples include a strong brand reputation, a loyal customer base, a talented workforce, or proprietary technology.
    *   **Weaknesses:** These are the internal factors that place the organization at a disadvantage relative to others. Examples include a weak brand, high levels of debt, an outdated technology stack, or a lack of a clear strategic direction.

*   **External Environment (Opportunities and Threats):** The external environment consists of factors outside the organization that are beyond its control.
    *   **Opportunities:** These are the external factors that the organization can leverage to its advantage. Examples include a growing market, favorable government policies, new technologies, or a competitor's weakness.
    *   **Threats:** These are the external factors that could potentially harm the organization. Examples include a new competitor, a shrinking market, changing customer preferences, or an economic downturn.

By examining these four areas, a company can develop strategies that leverage its strengths, mitigate its weaknesses, capitalize on opportunities, and defend against threats.

**a) Explain some common techniques used in Big data analytics.**

**Answer:**

Big data analytics is the process of examining large and varied data sets to uncover hidden patterns, unknown correlations, market trends, customer preferences, and other useful information. Some common techniques include:

1.  **Data Mining:** This is the process of discovering patterns and knowledge from large amounts of data. It involves using statistical techniques and machine learning algorithms to sift through data to identify trends and relationships.
2.  **Predictive Analytics:** This technique uses historical data, statistical algorithms, and machine learning techniques to identify the likelihood of future outcomes. For example, it can be used to predict customer churn or to identify which customers are most likely to respond to a marketing campaign.
3.  **Machine Learning:** This is a subset of artificial intelligence where algorithms are trained on data to learn and make predictions or decisions without being explicitly programmed. Common machine learning techniques used in big data analytics include regression, classification, and clustering.
4.  **Text Analytics (or Text Mining):** This involves analyzing unstructured text data from sources like social media, emails, and customer reviews to extract meaningful insights and sentiment.
5.  **A/B Testing:** Also known as split testing, this is a method of comparing two versions of a webpage, email, or other marketing asset to see which one performs better. While not exclusive to big data, it is often used in conjunction with big data analytics to optimize customer experiences at scale.

**b) Write short note on Structured and Unstructured Data.**

**Answer:**

**Structured Data:**

Structured data is data that is highly organized and formatted in a way that makes it easily searchable and analyzable by computers. It typically resides in relational databases and is often managed using SQL (Structured Query Language). The data is arranged in tables with rows and columns, and each column has a specific data type (e.g., number, text, date). Examples of structured data include customer information in a CRM database (name, address, phone number), sales transactions, and financial data.

**Unstructured Data:**

Unstructured data is data that does not have a pre-defined data model or is not organized in a pre-defined manner. It is often text-heavy, but can also include images, audio, and video. It is more difficult to process, analyze, and search than structured data. Examples of unstructured data include emails, social media posts, customer reviews, photos, videos, and documents. It is estimated that 80-90% of the data in any organization is unstructured. The rise of big data technologies has been largely driven by the need to analyze this vast amount of unstructured data.

**f) Enlist steps of Data Mining Procedure.**

**Answer:**

Data mining is the process of discovering patterns, trends, and useful information from large datasets. The procedure typically follows a set of steps, often referred to as the Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining (CRISP-DM). The key steps are:

1.  **Business Understanding:** This initial phase focuses on understanding the project objectives and requirements from a business perspective. The goal is to define the problem that the data mining project aims to solve (e.g., reduce customer churn, increase sales).
2.  **Data Understanding:** This phase involves collecting the initial data and getting familiar with it. This includes activities like loading the data, exploring its properties, and identifying any data quality issues.
3.  **Data Preparation:** This is often the most time-consuming phase. It involves cleaning, transforming, and formatting the data to make it suitable for the modeling phase. This can include handling missing values, removing duplicates, and creating new variables (feature engineering).
4.  **Modeling:** In this phase, various data mining techniques and algorithms (e.g., classification, clustering, regression) are selected and applied to the prepared data. The parameters of the models are calibrated to optimize performance.
5.  **Evaluation:** Before deploying the model, it is thoroughly evaluated to ensure it meets the business objectives. The results of the model are assessed for their accuracy and generalizability. This phase is about determining if the discovered patterns are valid and useful.
6.  **Deployment:** Once the model is evaluated and approved, it is deployed into the business environment so that it can be used to make decisions. This could involve integrating the model into a CRM system to provide real-time recommendations or creating a regular reporting process. The deployment phase also includes monitoring the model's performance over time.
